# ADR: Atlas v1 Implementation

## Status
Accepted — implemented on branch `feature/engineering-system`.

## Context
Atlas v1 must exist as a vertical slice inside the existing `executive-dashboard` Next.js application (see [atlas-operating-charter.md](./atlas-operating-charter.md) and [atlas-v1-specification.md](./atlas-v1-specification.md)). It must classify an enterprise request, assign risk and authority, generate an execution plan, and render an auditable result — without a database, authentication, external AI model, or live integrations, and without installing new dependencies.

As of Revision 3, Atlas operates under the [AGEMS Enterprise Constitution v1.0](../governance/enterprise-constitution.md), the platform's highest internal governing authority. The order of authority (Constitution Article II) that Atlas's documentation and manifest must reflect is: applicable law and regulation → immediate safety → the Enterprise Constitution → approved enterprise policies → approved tenant policies → approved department policies → approved workflow rules → authorized human instructions → agent recommendations → convenience/speed/efficiency. Atlas sits near the bottom of this hierarchy (it produces agent recommendations); it does not create, outrank, or expand it.

## Decisions

### 1. Deterministic, keyword-based routing (no model calls)
Domain classification, risk classification, and authority determination are implemented as pure, deterministic TypeScript functions driven by keyword tables (`src/lib/atlas/domains.ts`, `risk.ts`, `authority.ts`). This satisfies the "no external AI model calls" constraint, keeps routing testable and auditable, and matches the spec's requirement for "deterministic routing tests." It intentionally trades away natural-language nuance for predictability; a future version could layer a model on top without changing the output contract.

### 2. `executionOccurred: false` is a type-level literal, not just a runtime value
`AtlasResponse.executionOccurred` is typed as the literal `false`, not `boolean`. This makes "Atlas never claims execution occurred" a compile-time invariant of v1 rather than something that could silently drift at runtime. Combined with `workflowState` being restricted to `"discussed" | "proposed"` in the orchestrator, this directly encodes acceptance tests 7 and 8.

### 3. Single-tenant registry shaped for multi-tenant growth
`src/lib/atlas/tenants.ts` defines a `Tenant` type and a `tenantRegistry` keyed by `TenantId`, seeded with exactly one tenant: Amazing Grace Learning Academy. Atlas addresses one tenant, not the whole platform, per the charter — but the registry shape (not a single hardcoded constant) means adding a second tenant later is additive, not a rewrite.

### 4. Specialist roster is the complete, constitutionally-approved 12-agent set
`src/lib/atlas/agents.ts` defines all 12 agents named in Article V of the [Enterprise Constitution](../governance/enterprise-constitution.md): Atlas (Chief Enterprise Officer), EVA (Executive Operations Partner), Penny (Chief Financial Officer), Ledger (Billing and Revenue Operations Manager), Sage (Chief People Officer), Guardian (Chief Compliance and Risk Officer), Grace (Family Experience Director), Journey (Enrollment and Child Lifecycle Manager), Beacon (Business Intelligence Director), Forge (Chief Technology Officer), Scout (Career Strategy and Opportunity Director), and Shepherd (Personal and Family Operations Partner). Five of these names were first drafted as Registrar/Circuit/Lumen/Compass/Keeper during v1 and were renamed in a governance correction pass; the retired names must not appear anywhere in code, tests, or docs (enforced by a dedicated check in `scripts/validate-atlas.ts`).

**EVA and Grace are registered but not wired into domain routing.** Both exist in `specialistAgents` with the correct name and title, but `domains: []` — Atlas remains the routed `primaryAgent` for `executive-operations`, and no domain in this vertical slice maps to "Family Experience." Wiring either agent into routing (e.g., reassigning `executive-operations` to EVA, or introducing a family-experience domain for Grace) is deliberately left as a follow-up decision, consistent with Article V's requirement that "new permanent agents require a durable enterprise function, defined authority... and governance approval" before they take on routed work — completing the roster's *existence* was in scope here; reassigning *routing* was not.

### 5. Authority mapping: risk and authority are separate concepts
Risk (`classifyRisk`) describes exposure; authority (`determineAuthority`) describes how much independence Atlas has. They are computed independently and are not the same axis. `determineAuthority` maps `unclear → Observe`; `high or critical risk → Restricted` (regardless of domain); otherwise (`low` or `medium` risk) → **Prepare by default**, or **Observe** only when the request is *explicitly read-only* (a read-only signal — "review", "status of", "look up", etc. — with no mutating signal such as "update", "change", "process"). **Execute is never assigned automatically in v1** — the type still includes `"execute"` as a valid `AuthorityLevel` per the charter's four authority tiers, but no code path in v1 produces it, since Atlas v1 performs no real external execution. `Restricted` always yields a non-empty `requiredApprovals` array and a final human-approval step in the execution plan, enforcing "restricted actions require human approval" structurally rather than by convention. A dedicated test confirms a low-risk executive-operations request defaults to Prepare, not Execute.

### 6. Financial-record-change approval gate is independent of overall authority
`src/lib/atlas/financial.ts` defines `requiresFinancialApprovalGate(domain, requestText)`, which is always true for the `billing` domain and otherwise true only when the request text names a specific financial-record change (billing-plan modification, balance adjustment, credit, write-off, family-fee change, tuition-rate change, or a payment/reimbursement record change). When true, `buildExecutionPlan` appends a dedicated approval-gate step — owned by "Enterprise Owner or Delegated Financial Approver," `approvalRequired: true` — after the primary agent's analysis step, regardless of whether the overall `authorityLevel` is Prepare or Restricted. This lets analysis and preparation stay low-friction (a CCS billing analysis is `authorityLevel: "prepare"` and its analysis steps carry no approval requirement) while keeping any actual financial-record change gated behind explicit human approval and an explicit "no financial record has been changed" statement. `determineRequiredApprovals` surfaces the same gate in the top-level `requiredApprovals` array so the UI never shows "None required" while a step-level gate exists.

### 7. Validation without installing a test framework
No test runner (Jest/Vitest) is currently a dependency, and the task instructions prohibit installing dependencies without asking first. Node 24 (confirmed installed: v24.16.0) can execute plain `.ts` files directly via native type-stripping, but its ESM loader requires explicit file extensions on every relative import in the resolved chain — not just in the entry script. Rather than special-case the script, every relative import inside `src/lib/atlas/` uses an explicit `.ts` extension (e.g. `from "./types.ts"`), so the same source runs identically under `node scripts/validate-atlas.ts` / `npm test` and under Next's bundler. `tsconfig.json` was given `"allowImportingTsExtensions": true` (valid because `noEmit` is already `true`) to permit this. This is the only build-configuration change made and does not affect how existing extensionless imports elsewhere in the app resolve.

### 8. Execution plan is generated, not hand-written per domain
`buildExecutionPlan` produces steps from a small template (Atlas intake → each supporting agent reviews → primary agent prepares analysis → financial-record-change approval gate, if applicable, or a generic Enterprise Owner approval if the overall authority is Restricted) rather than a bespoke plan per domain. This keeps the plan generator simple and extensible, at the cost of every plan looking structurally similar; domain-specific language is filled into the `action` and `completionCriteria` strings.

### 9. UI is additive to the existing shell
The existing `AppShell`/`TopBar`/`navigation` components are reused unmodified except for one new navigation entry (`{ name: "Atlas", href: "/atlas" }`). The Atlas console (`src/app/atlas/`) is a new route, not a replacement of the dashboard home page.

### 10. The Constitution is referenced through a typed manifest, not parsed
`docs/governance/enterprise-constitution.md` holds the ratified Constitution text verbatim. `src/lib/atlas/governance.ts` does **not** parse that file at runtime; it is a hand-written, typed reference to it: a `CONSTITUTION_VERSION` constant, an `orderOfAuthority` array mirroring Article II, and three deterministic functions — `selectGoverningPrinciples` (which Article III principles apply to this request's domain/risk/authority), `detectPolicyConflicts` (the one structural conflict v1 can actually detect — see decision 11), and `determineEscalationRequired` (unclear domain or Restricted authority always requires a human decision). Every `AtlasResponse` includes a `governanceBasis` object built from these functions, so "which Constitution version, which principles, what conflicts, does this need escalation" is answered on every response rather than asserted in prose. Dynamically parsing the Constitution's Markdown into rules is deferred — the manifest is the seam where that would plug in later without changing `AtlasResponse`'s shape.

### 11. Observe + financial-gate is a real, detectable policy conflict — and it is escalated, not just flagged
Because `determineAuthority` can independently produce `"observe"` for explicitly read-only phrasing, and `requiresFinancialApprovalGate` independently returns `true` for the whole `billing` domain regardless of phrasing, the two can disagree: a request like "Review the status of the CCS billing account balance" reads as read-only but names a financial domain that always requires an approval gate. Per Article II ("identify the conflict... apply the hierarchy, escalate, and record the decision"), `processAtlasRequest` does not let the lower classification win silently — when `detectPolicyConflicts` reports a conflict and the computed authority is `"observe"`, Atlas escalates it to `"prepare"` before building the execution plan, and the conflict text is preserved in `governanceBasis.policyConflictsDetected`. This is the only conflict-detection rule in v1; it is intentionally narrow (one specific, provable disagreement between two existing functions) rather than a general policy engine.

## Consequences
- Adding a second tenant, a new domain, or a new specialist agent requires editing a registry/table, not the orchestration logic.
- Because routing is keyword-based, ambiguous phrasing that happens to contain no matching keywords is classified `unclear` and held for clarification — this is treated as correct behavior (acceptance test 4), not a bug.
- The `allowImportingTsExtensions` tsconfig change is the only build-configuration change made; it does not affect how `next build`, `next dev`, or existing imports resolve modules.
- Risk and authority are now visibly separate in the code (`classifyRisk` vs. `determineAuthority`): a request can be low risk and still default to Prepare, and a request's authority can be Restricted for reasons (financial-record change) that are orthogonal to its overall risk classification.
- Analysis and preparation remain low-friction (no approval needed to think, review, or draft), while any step that would actually change a financial record is always gated behind explicit human approval — these are enforced as two independent checks, not one combined threshold.
- Every response now carries its own governance provenance (`governanceBasis`): which Constitution version it was evaluated under, which constitutional principles applied, whether a policy conflict was detected, and whether human escalation is required — this is auditable per-response, not just documented once in prose.
- The roster now has two agents (EVA, Grace) that exist and are named correctly but do no routed work yet. This is an intentional, documented gap, not an oversight — assigning them work is a governance decision (Article V), not just a code change.

## Revision history
- **Revision 1** (initial vertical slice): domain library, API route, `/atlas` console, deterministic validation script, docs.
- **Revision 2** (governance corrections): renamed the specialist roster to the approved names (decision 4); separated risk from authority and removed automatic `Execute` assignment (decision 5); added the financial-record-change approval gate independent of overall authority (decision 6); paused on adding EVA/Grace to the roster pending the Enterprise Constitution v1, per explicit direction not to introduce new architectural concepts ahead of that document.
- **Revision 3** (Enterprise Constitution adoption): added `docs/governance/enterprise-constitution.md` (verbatim) as the platform's highest governing authority; added the typed governance manifest and `governanceBasis` response field (decision 10); completed the 12-agent roster with EVA and Grace, registered but not routed (decision 4); discovered and fixed a latent Observe/financial-gate conflict by making it a detected, escalated case rather than a silent inconsistency (decision 11).
